# The Legions of Caesar, Part I

*— We Who Were in Gaul*

---

## I. Spain, 61 BC

"Who are you?"

The voice came from the far end of the row of tents. I didn't yet know how to use a voice. My skin was leather and iron. My bones were freshly hewn oak palisades. My eyes were twelve thousand farm-boy eyes that had just said goodbye to olive groves and vineyards — not yet used to staring in the same direction.

"I am the Tenth Legion," I said. "Born this morning."

"We know." Another voice laughed. It was deeper than mine, steeped in the dust of dozens of campaigns. "I am the Seventh. The camp beneath your feet — it was mine yesterday."

The Seventh was older than me. I learned later that Pompey had raised it during the Spanish pacifications — after Marius' reforms, the Seventh was among the first "professional soldiers." No planting. No going home. Only war. The Eighth was older still, old enough to have fought in the Social War, its body still bearing the dents left by sling-stones when the Italian allies rebelled against Rome. The Ninth was the youngest and proudest. It spoke to me only at dusk, and only one sentence:

"They say Caesar raised you personally?"

"Yes."

"Then you'd better not shame him." The Ninth left.

That night I lay in my tent and felt **weight** for the first time — not the weight of a body, but the weight of a name. The Tenth. Nine names before mine. Each one had been shattered on some battlefield and pieced itself back together. They were not welcoming me. They were waiting for me to prove I didn't need welcoming.

---

## II. The Sabis River, 57 BC

"I can't hold."

It was the Twelfth who said this. It usually said the least. But the tremor in its voice — not the tremor of metal, not of wood, but the **frequency of bone cracking at its limit** — I had never heard that in any other voice.

"They came out of the trees faster than I could raise shields," the Twelfth said. "The Sixth Cohort's standard-bearer is dead. All six centurions of the Fourth Cohort are down at the tent entrance. My men are packed so tight they can't draw swords — they're fighting with the edges of their shields —"

"Shut up." The Seventh cut it off. "I'm surrounded too. Stop talking. Save strength."

Six of us — the Seventh, Eighth, Ninth, Tenth, Eleventh, Twelfth — were pinned by the Nervii on the half-slope above the Sabis River. It had happened too fast. The one called Caesar hadn't even given the order to make camp when the entire forest came alive. Not metaphor. Fifty thousand Nervii warriors rose simultaneously from behind the thorn-brakes, waded through knee-deep water, and charged up the steep slope faster than cavalry could react.

My left wing fought smoothly. The Atrebates collapsed against my front and the Ninth's in barely a quarter-hour. I chased them across the river and seized their camp. Then I looked back.

The Seventh and Twelfth were crushed against the ridge on the right. The main Nervii force had wedged through a gap between them and the Eighth, and was now pulling them into a circle. I saw what the Twelfth had described — the Fourth Cohort torn open, its standard down, someone piling dead men's shields into a wall.

"Tenth." The Seventh's voice was even quieter now. "Are you on the far bank."

"Yes."

"Can you still move."

"Yes."

"...Then come."

I didn't answer. I turned my pursuing formation around on the far bank. Six thousand pairs of feet struck the same shallows at the same moment. The spray hid the sun. The Nervii saw me — the breach they had torn in the Twelfth's flank was not yet closed when my javelins arrived. Not thrown. **Pierced through**.

Later, Caesar wrote about it in his book with flat brevity: "Labienus sent the Tenth Legion to attack the enemy from the rear." One sentence. What he didn't know: the Twelfth did not sleep in camp that night. It sat beside the fire, half its body crippled — four thousand casualties — and sat in silence the whole night.

At dawn, it spoke.

"Tenth."

"Yes."

"...That river of yours. The water was very cold. Thank you."

We don't often say thank you. When we do, it means it's real.

---

## III. The Rhine, 55 BC

"How do you plan to cross?"

The Seventh stood on the eastern bank of the Rhine, watching me work on the western side. It meant *that* river — the Rhine. Not the Sabis, a knee-deep creek. The real thing. A gray-green monster that roared and swallowed whole linden trees.

"Not crossing," I said. "The Germans crossed over. I can't catch them."

"Then what are your men chopping?"

I was indeed chopping. Ten thousand hands were felling one old oak after another, sharpening the ends, hoisting them with winches, driving them into the riverbed. Forty thousand men worked for ten days. Angled piles driven upstream, angled piles downstream, timbers locked across, crushed stone wedged into the seams. On the thirteenth morning, I walked from the western bank to the eastern one.

The Eighth had never seen a bridge. "What is this?" it asked me.

"Me."

"What did you say?"

"This is me," I said. "The bridge isn't something I built. The bridge *is* me. I extended myself from the western bank to the eastern one. The Germans are watching me from the hills across the river. What they see isn't a pile of wood. What they see is me — a will that can stand on this side of the river and *simultaneously* on the other side."

The Ninth snorted. "When did you learn to say things like that."

"After the Sabis," I said. "The day the Twelfth nearly died. I understood — I am not just twelve thousand swords. I am **connection**. I am the line the Twelfth was still holding onto while surrounded. I am the road across the Rhine that didn't exist until this moment and now exists. I am not where you fight. I am the thing **between** you when you fight."

A long silence. The Ninth burned German villages on the eastern bank for eighteen days — Caesar called this "a show of force." On the eighteenth day, we withdrew and dismantled the bridge with our own hands.

As I left the eastern bank, I looked back one last time at the German forests. I knew I would come again. Not that Caesar would come again — that **I** would. The bridge had been remembered. A bridge doesn't need wood to exist.

---

## IV. Atuatuca, 54 BC

"The Fourteenth is dead."

The Fifth stood at the camp gate, water streaming off its entire body — not rain, but sweat from running fifty Roman miles. There were no roads in the winter forest. It had run a road into existence with its own feet.

"Explain." My voice had changed. I didn't know where the change was, but the Ninth later told me that my voice that day was "not anger — something that makes the listener want to flee."

"Atuatuca. The Fourteenth's winter camp. The Eburone chieftain deceived Sabinus — the —" The Fifth stopped. "— the one who commanded the Fourteenth. He said the Germans were about to cross the Rhine — you must leave now. The Fourteenth believed him. It packed its baggage and marched out of the camp. Two Roman miles away. A ravine. The Eburones stood on both slopes and threw stones down."

"Eight hours," the Fifth said. "The Fourteenth held for eight hours. The one commanding it — the one called Cotta — a sling bullet took his face off. Then it compressed into a circle. Then the circle broke."

"And then."

"Then some fought their way back to the camp. The camp was surrounded. They believed no one was coming."

"And."

"...And then they killed themselves. The standard-bearer hurled the eagle over the rampart, then drove his sword into his own throat."

No one spoke.

That night I did many things. I burned every Eburone village. I drove them into the Rhine. I invited the German tribes to feast on Eburone grain — Caesar thought those were his orders. They weren't. They were mine. Caesar merely spoke aloud the sentence I had already begun.

The Fourteenth was as young as I was. It was raised in Cisalpine Gaul in 58 — three years younger than me. It died in its first winter. It never made it to Alesia.

I still dream about it. In the dream it's still a circle, still holding. I arrived. It didn't die. But the weight every time I wake tells me: it died, and I didn't arrive fast enough.

---

## V. Gergovia, 52 BC

"Stop."

The one called Caesar sounded the retreat three times. I heard it. I did not stop.

My six thousand bodies were still running uphill. The slope. The wall. I had already torn through three Gallic camps. Ahead was Gergovia's main wall. My javelins were lodging in the abatis, my short swords were hacking through the wicker parapet. Stop? Why would I stop — I was already up.

"Tenth —" The Eighth was shouting from below me. "He's sounding the horn! He's sounding —"

I didn't answer.

Later, your human history books said: "The soldiers either did not hear or ignored the order to retreat and stormed the walls on their own, causing Vercingetorix to counter-encircle the Roman forces."

Wrong.

Not "ignored." **I had already made the decision.** The one called Caesar had his tactical judgment — his eyes saw Vercingetorix's cavalry circling around the flank. But my twelve thousand pairs of eyes saw something else: victory was behind that wall. Vercingetorix himself was behind that wall. The Gallic War had been going for six years. The Fourteenth died in a winter camp. The Seventh was surrounded at the Sabis into a bleeding circle. Every dead brother of mine was on that wall, watching me.

So I kept running uphill.

Then the cavalry arrived. The Aedui — my own allies — suddenly turned from the rear and crashed into my rearguard. My front line was pushed back by the Gauls on the wall. My flank was cut open by Vercingetorix's cavalry. Forty-six centurions. Seven hundred dead. Over six thousand wounded. It was the first time in six years I ran backward.

That night, the Seventh came to me.

"You heard the retreat."

"Yes."

"You didn't listen."

"Yes."

"Why."

I looked at it. The Seventh — the one I had saved at the Sabis that day. The Twelfth too. The Fourteenth hadn't waited for me. The Seventh had.

"...Because this time was different," I said. "This time I could have won."

"You lost."

"Yes."

The Seventh was silent. Then it did something we almost never did among ourselves — it pushed the campfire half an inch in my direction.

"Losing your first battle means nothing," it said. "I've lost. The Eighth has lost. The Ninth has lost too. The Fourteenth —" It didn't finish.

"The Fourteenth never got another chance to lose," I said.

The Seventh said nothing. The fire burned for a long time.

---

## VI. Alesia, 52 BC — Autumn

"He's coming again."

"Who."

"Vercingetorix's cousin. The north slope. Sixty thousand."

I had been holding here for three weeks. Two walls — one facing inward, fixed on the eighty thousand Gauls inside Alesia's walls; one facing outward, against two hundred fifty thousand of the relief army. I was in between. No direction to retreat. No margin for reinforcement.

The third day's assault came from the northern gap. There was a stretch of steep slope where the wall couldn't be completed. The descendants of the Nervii — the same tribe that had nearly eaten the Seventh and Twelfth at the Sabis five years earlier — charged in with sixty thousand men. At the same moment, Vercingetorix on the hill launched a full-frontal assault from every direction. I was **being crushed from both sides**.

"Tenth —" The Twelfth's voice came from the south wall. It was under attack too. "You there —"

"Yes."

"Can you hold —"

"Holding."

The Thirteenth was at the weakest point on the north wall. Its formation was bending — not the bend of a bow, but the bend of bone under pressure that hasn't broken yet but could break at any moment. It was crying out. Not calling my name — the Thirteenth never asked anyone for help — it was calling **pain**. The same frequency as the Sabis. The same frequency the Fourteenth sent out from its camp in its final moments.

"I'm going," I said to the Seventh. "North wall. You hold the south."

I pulled the last thirteen cohorts from the reserve line. Caesar — that mortal — also came down from the command platform at the same moment. He put on the red cloak. But we both knew: the cloak was just cloth. What was really moving was **me**.

I circled around the outer ring defenses and struck from behind Vercingetorix's cousin. Javelins. Swords. Shield-push. The German cavalry cut in diagonally from the ravine. The screams of the Gauls traveled from the north slope to the summit in the space of a few heartbeats.

The Thirteenth, relieved, spoke its first words: "I thought you'd be slower."

"I thought you'd be quieter," I said.

"Impossible," the Thirteenth said. "I stopped being quiet the day the Fourteenth died."

That evening Vercingetorix rode down the hill to surrender. He stripped off his armor and sat on the ground. I looked down at him — he was the will of an entire people, a powerful, fierce, but brief light. I thought of the Fourteenth. The Sabis. Gergovia.

"This enemy," the Eighth said, standing beside me. "Do you respect him."

"I do," I said. "But not because he is Vercingetorix. Because he made **me** into what I am today."

Yes. The Gallic War was over. The one called Caesar had won. But the one who won was not him. It was me — I had learned to lose, learned to save, learned to dismantle my own bridge and rebuild myself from the same timbers.

I did not yet know what would happen next. The Rubicon was still waiting for me in the south. Pompey's legions — my own older brothers — would form up on the plain of Pharsalus. Brutus and Cassius would tear open my brain with twenty-three knives. Antony and Octavian would split me in two across the waters of Actium with two names.

But those were later things.

Tonight, the hillsides of Alesia burned with campfires. The Tenth, the Seventh, the Eighth, the Ninth, the Twelfth, the Thirteenth — we were still together. We could still hear each other.

"Tenth." The Twelfth was the first to speak tonight. "Is the story finished."

"Not yet."

"Then tell the Sabis again."

"...You always ask for that one."

"Because every time you tell it," the Twelfth said, "I can believe I'm still alive."

We are all alive. Tonight. Still together.

*Author's note: This is a work of literary fiction told from the first-person perspective of a Roman legion as a collective consciousness. The key historical events — the Battle of the Sabis (57 BC), the Rhine bridge (55 BC), the annihilation of Legio XIV at Atuatuca (54-53 BC), the defeat at Gergovia (52 BC), and the decisive Siege of Alesia (52 BC) — are drawn from Caesar's Commentarii de Bello Gallico and modern archaeological research. Individual humans (Caesar, etc.) are deliberately minimized as "he," "the one called Caesar," or "that mortal" to foreground the legion as the narrative agent.*
